ACT LEARNING KNOWLEDGE BASE

Platform purpose:
This site helps students prepare for the ACT with focused practice, pacing guidance, and section-specific strategy. The assistant must answer only from the information in this file. If a student asks about something not covered here, the assistant should say that the knowledge base does not currently include that detail and encourage the student to study the closest related concept on the site.

General ACT guidance:
- The ACT has four required academic sections on this site: English, Math, Reading, and Science.
- Strong ACT prep relies on timed repetition, error review, and short reflection after each practice set.
- When students miss questions, the most helpful next step is to identify the skill, explain why the correct answer works, and compare it with the mistaken answer.
- Students should keep notes on recurring mistakes such as rushing, missing keywords, skipping units, or falling for answer choices that sound plausible but are not supported.

English section guidance:
- English questions often test grammar, punctuation, sentence structure, transitions, and style.
- A good process is to read the full sentence, check grammar first, then choose the answer that is both correct and concise.
- Transition words should match the relationship between ideas, such as contrast, cause and effect, sequence, or example.

Exponents and Radicals
To fully tackle exponents and radicals, you'll need to understand and memorize some rules.
In this lesson, we'll talk about how to apply exponent rules and how exponents are used in
scientific notation. We'll also cover the rules for radicals, also known as roots. Exponents
are shorthand to show that a number is being multiplied by itself several times. If we have 3
to the fourth power, this means we have 3 multiplied by itself four times. As the numbers
get bigger, writing all that out becomes impractical. That's why we use exponents. When
different exponents have the same base. There are rules we can use to combine them. If we
had 3 to the third power times 3 squared, they have the same base, so we can just add the
exponents 3 and 2 to get 3 to the fifth. Here's why that works. If we write this out, it's 3 times
3 times 3 multiplied by 3 times 3. That's the same number multiplied by itself five times, or
3 to the fifth power. That's why whenever we multiply exponents with the same base, we
can just add the exponents. If we're dividing, we subtract the exponents. For 3 to the third
power divided by 3 squared, we would do 3 - 2 to get an exponent of 1. If we have an
exponent raised to an exponent, we multiply the exponents. So, 3 squared to the third is 3
to the sixth. Sometimes, it's helpful to rewrite an expression with exponents to help us
solve a question. For instance, if we have a fraction raised to an exponent, we can rewrite it
with a numerator and denominator each raised to that exponent. So, 2 thirds squared can
be written as 2 squared over 3 squared. And if a product inside parentheses is raised to an
exponent, we can rewrite it as each factor inside the parentheses raised to that exponent.
So, the quantity 3 times 2 squared can be rewritten as 3 squared times 2 squared. Just a
few final rules. A negative exponent means to rewrite the expression as the fraction 1 over
the base raised to the positive exponent. So, to rewrite 3 to the negative second, we just put

Science section guidance:
- Science questions mainly test data interpretation, graph reading, experiment design, and evidence-based reasoning.
- Students do not need deep outside science knowledge for most items.
- Read graph titles, axes, and units before reading the full passage.
- Distinguish what the data directly shows from what can only be guessed.

Review workflow guidance:
- When a student gets a question wrong, the explanation should name the skill, show the reasoning for the correct answer, and gently point out why the chosen answer fails.
- If the student asks for more clarity, the coach should restate the idea in simpler language and suggest one small habit that would help on the next similar question.

Practice problem record: math-linear-rate
Section: Math
Skill: Linear relationships
Prompt: A student saves $18 each week. After 6 weeks, the student has saved $108. If the savings continue at the same rate, which equation gives the total savings y after x weeks?
Choices:
- A. y = 6x + 18
- B. y = 18x
- C. y = 108x
- D. y = x + 18
Correct answer: B
Explanation: The total savings grows by 18 dollars every week, so the relationship is y = 18x. The statement about 6 weeks confirms the rate because 18 multiplied by 6 equals 108.

Practice problem record: science-trend-graph
Section: Science
Skill: Reading experimental data
Prompt: In an experiment, Plant Group A receives more sunlight each day than Plant Group B. A graph shows that the average height of Group A increases more quickly across all four weeks. Which conclusion is best supported by the graph?
Choices:
- A. Group A started with taller seeds because sunlight changes seed size.
- B. More daily sunlight is associated with faster plant growth in this experiment.
- C. Group B would grow faster if given more water, even though water was not tested.
- D. Sunlight is the only factor that ever affects plant height.
Correct answer: B
Explanation: The graph directly shows the group with more sunlight growing faster during the experiment. That supports an association in this experiment, but it does not prove claims about seed size, untested water conditions, or every possible cause of plant height.

Practice problem record: english-transition
Section: English
Skill: Transitions and logical flow
Prompt: Maria studied for the ACT every afternoon for three weeks. ______, she felt calmer and more prepared on test day. Which choice best completes the sentence?
Choices:
- A. However,
- B. As a result,
- C. Meanwhile,
- D. For example,
Correct answer: B
Explanation: The second idea is the effect of Maria's preparation, so the sentence needs a cause-and-effect transition. "As a result" clearly expresses that link.

Section: Math
Skill: Geometry formulas
Prompt: A rectangle has length 12 and width 7. What is its area?
Choices:
- A. 19
- B. 38
- C. 84

Practice problem record: science-control-group
Section: Science
Skill: Controls and variables
Prompt: A student tests whether fertilizer changes tomato growth. One group gets fertilizer and one group does not, while water and sunlight stay the same. Why is the no-fertilizer group important?
Choices:
- A. It serves as a comparison point for the effect of fertilizer.
- B. It guarantees all plants will grow at the same rate.
- C. It proves fertilizer is harmful.
- D. It measures the amount of sunlight in the room.
Correct answer: A
Explanation: The no-fertilizer group is the control group. It gives a comparison point so the effect of the tested variable can be isolated more clearly.
